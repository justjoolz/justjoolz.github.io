01York · early years

Noise before names

I grew up in the first home-computer era. My dad was an unusually early adopter, so computers arrived in our house when they still felt less like appliances and more like small, temperamental portals. The Tandy TRS-80 was part of that world, evidence that my father had reached the future early.

I learnt BASIC, then bits of C++ and Pascal. None of it seemed related to music. Music happened elsewhere: in the kitchen, where pots became a drum kit; in a nylon-string guitar with a microphone pushed inside it; in my dad’s Sony reel-to-reel, which could turn an innocent sound into something fierce and distorted. I was interested in what happened when an ordinary thing was wired incorrectly on purpose.

There was no plan and very little technique. There was only the thrill of making a sound that had not existed a moment earlier, then leaning closer to hear what it suggested next. I did not have language for this way of working. I just knew the sounds were usually more interesting than my ideas about them.

02York · bands

Bass, sweat and volume

At primary school I picked up basic guitar chords and blues scales. The first cassettes I bought were Michael Jackson’s Bad and a MegaMix dance compilation, which is a reasonably accurate map of the territory even now. I recorded the radio, talked over it and made mixtapes for friends and family. Nobody had asked for a radio presenter in the house, but that did not seem important.

By secondary school I had moved through rock ’n’ roll, punk and heavy metal. The consistent principle was maximum volume. My drummer and guitarist friends and I rehearsed as if loudness might compensate for the absence of a method. To be fair, it often did. I learnt a great deal by watching the drummer who would later play in Seed, especially about how rhythm could carry an entire room.

I found my way to bass without classical training and bought a Fender Precision. Bass made sense physically before it made sense intellectually. Seed became known around York, playing packed nights at Fibbers and supporting bands passing through. I liked the stage, but more than that I liked the moment a group of separate people became one machine—sweaty, unstable and briefly much larger than its parts.

03York · machines arrive

The electronic awakening

The turn came when I saw Charlie Floyd perform live electronic music in Subway featuring the Mighty Mossadon. There were flashing lights, patterns being altered in real time and a crowd reacting to the removal of a single kick drum. I had spent years around computers and years around music, but I had somehow kept them in different mental rooms. That night somebody knocked the wall down.

Propellerhead ReBirth gave me a virtual TB-303, 808 and 909 before owning the originals was remotely plausible. I could reach into a repeating pattern and change its temperature while it was moving. The tiny adjustments people call tweaking did not feel tiny from inside the sound. A filter turn could make tension accumulate; a mute could create a space the crowd filled with anticipation.

I eventually bought a Roland MC-303 Groovebox. It meant I could perform without dragging a desktop computer to a venue, and I spent hours watching its lights and building grooves. It was a limited machine, which was part of its value. You had to learn its habits, work around its refusals and discover the accidents it was unusually good at producing.

04York · underground

Records and free parties

A spontaneous dub reggae jam beside the river introduced another idea: repetition could be meditative rather than merely repetitive. Deep bass did not need to demand attention. It could make a foundation strong enough for attention to move freely. That understanding has stayed with me through every change of genre and machine.

At York’s Arts Centre I watched a DJ do much more than join records together. He cut, spliced and worked the EQ until the mixer became an instrument. I bought my first vinyl—Tipper’s Music For SoundSystems—and then bought records with the focus of somebody developing an expensive storage problem.

I carried a bag of them into house parties and free parties where I knew nobody. Often I did not own the decks and had not been invited to play. I would wait, listen and eventually ask if I could have a go. It was not an efficient career strategy, but it was an excellent education: unfamiliar equipment, impatient rooms and no time to build a carefully managed impression.

05York → Nottingham

HEDST8 and The Bomb

Through solo sets and acid-and-breaks experiments I became close friends with DJ Nik Bryan. We formed the JNK collective, and later, with Ben “Ginger,” became HEDST8. The point was not to defend a genre. Drum & bass, house, breakbeat and bootleg logic were all material. If a combination had energy—if it had what Nik and I called ~ness—we followed it.

The studio grew to include an MPC2000 and Korg MS2000R. HEDST8 played Breakdown at The Bomb in Nottingham, a club whose sound system made detail feel architectural. We worked our way into the main room and played on a bill supporting Andy Cato of Groove Armada. The specificity matters more than dressing it up: it was a serious room, with a serious system, and being trusted with it meant something.

One day I bought an Eminem acapella, made a drum & bass remix and performed it that evening at The Bomb. That sequence—find something alive, work quickly, test it in public—contains more truth about the period than a list of adjectives ever could.

06Europe → Southeast Asia

Islands and migrations

Music took me outward. A Menorca trip led to time at Cova d’en Xoroi; later came Malaysia, Thailand and island residencies whose exact sequence still needs sorting out from photographs, flyers and other people’s memories. The useful thing about an archive is that it can admit uncertainty. I would rather write “around then” than give a confident date that happens to be wrong.

I arrived in Thailand with a flight case of tunes and a laptop, found a New Year’s Eve gig and, soon after, played at the Full Moon Party in Haad Rin. I became resident across two islands. Moving through remote places made the weight and fragility of vinyl difficult to ignore. CDs gave way to software, and Ableton Live became a practical performance instrument on a Dell XPS1210 bought while I was teaching English in Korea.

The format changed; the attraction did not. I still wanted to touch the structure while it was playing—to extend the right moment, remove the obvious element and feel when a room was ready for the next thing.

07Malaysia · from 2012

Music goes underground

From 2012, based in Malaysia, the public musical story became quieter. That does not mean music vanished. It went underground: private production, occasional mixes, long pauses and a life that did not arrange itself around maintaining an entertainment-industry narrative.

My involvement with Vajrayana Buddhism and meditation deepened. I am a practitioner, not a spiritual teacher, and this site should not confuse those roles. Practice did, however, give me language for things I had already recognised in music: the intelligence of not forcing, the instability of fixed identity, the way repetition can reveal difference rather than erase it.

Quiet periods are usually removed from biographies as if nothing valid can happen without public output. I do not want to do that here. Some work needed privacy. Some work was simply unfinished. Some years had other demands. The gaps are part of the rhythm.

08Hanoi → Đà Nẵng

Vietnam and return

I moved to Hanoi and later Đà Nẵng. Around 2020, Metagranulations emerged from that period; in 2021 came Stylin’ ft. Cutty Ranks, still my favourite completed production. Fatherhood and other changes did not produce a cinematic comeback. They made the available time more precious and the reasons for using it clearer.

Technology work continued alongside music. I found myself designing software and protocols using instincts that felt suspiciously familiar: find the underlying pulse, reduce unnecessary control, create conditions in which complex behaviour can emerge, then pay close attention to what the system is actually doing rather than what the diagram promised.

The return to sustained creativity has been gradual and unforced. That is probably why it feels durable. There is no need to manufacture continuity between every earlier phase. The work can disappear, surface in another country, change its tools and still belong to the same inquiry.

09Now · still listening

Sound as practice

Recent pieces have been built from mantra, dhāraṇī and lineage recordings. The Mañjuśrī piece appeared in 2024, inspired by going to see my teacher, who is said to be a manifestation of Mañjuśrī—the music an ornament to the dhāraṇī as chanted.

This work requires care: attention to source, permission, context and the difference between practice material and aesthetic decoration. Buddhism is not a visual moodboard, and I am not interested in using sacred material to make ordinary electronic music appear profound.

I still begin in the same place I did with kitchen objects, a reel-to-reel and no formal method. I listen to what is present. I wait for the part with energy. Then I follow it until the work reveals enough of its logic to continue without me forcing it. The machines are better now. The listening is the work.
